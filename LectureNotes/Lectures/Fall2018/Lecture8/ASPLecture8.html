    Resetting: Suppose a process drifts up at speed 1 and jumps back to 0 at rate 1.

    The generator is then:

    dd⁢x⁢f⁢(x)+(f⁢(0)-f⁢(x))

    Bathtub: Suppose a bath is filling at a speed of 1 gal/min, but a kid jumps in at independent random intervals with Exponential(1) distribution and splashes out a random, uniformly chosen proportion of water.

    dd⁢x⁢f⁢(x)+1x⁢∫0x(f⁢(y)-f⁢(x))⁢𝑑y

3 Markov Chains

Lemma 3.1 (Generating functions of paths).

Let A=(Ai⁢j)i,j∈[n], where [n]={1,2,…,n}, and let 𝒫i,jk={paths of length k from i to j}={P∈[n]k+1:p0=i,pk=j} (including self-loops). Then,

(Ak)i⁢j=∑P∈𝒫i,jk∏ℓ=1kAPℓ-1,Pℓ
Proof.

(By induction.)

Base Case(s): When k=0, (A0)i⁢j={1,i=j0,else. When k=1, (A1)i⁢j=Ai⁢j.

Inductive Step: Suppose that the assumption holds for k. Then

(Ak+1)i⁢j =(Ak⁢A)i⁢j
=∑m=1n(Ak)i⁢m⁢Am⁢j
=∑m=1n(∑𝒫i,mk∏ℓ=1kAPℓ-1,Pℓ)⁢Am⁢j
=∑𝒫i,mk+1∏ℓ=1k+1APℓ-1,Pℓ

∎

Discrete-Time Markov Chains (DTMC)

Let (Mk)k∈ℕ≥0 be a DTMC with n states, labelled by [n], i.e. for x,y∈[n] the transition probability is ℙ⁢{Mk+1=y∣Mk=x,M≤k}=ℙ⁢{Mk+1=y∣Mk=x}=Px⁢y. Then,

ℙ⁢{Mk+ℓ=y∣Mk=x}=(Pℓ)x⁢y

Proof. (By the above lemma.)

(Note: Px⁢x can be greater than 0.)

Discrete-time Markov chains can do things that continuous-time ones can’t, namely that they can oscillate/bounce up and down.

The Poissonization of M

Example 3.2.

Let Nt=N⁢([0,t]) be a Poisson (counting) process on t∈(0,∞) of constant rate 1, and let Xt=MNt, i.e. Xt= “location of M after Nt jumps” (independently of (Mk)k≥0).

Question: Is X a Markov process? (Yes.)

Solution.

Let J=Nt+s-Nt= # of jumps in (t,t+s]∼P⁢o⁢i⁢s⁢(s), independent of (X≤t). Then,

(Xt+s∣Xt=x) =(MNt+J∣MNt=x)
=d(MJ∣M0=x), by the Markov property of M,

which is completely independent of what happened before time t. Thus, it is a Markov process.

Corollary 3.3 (transition probability).

ℙ⁢{Xt+s=y∣Xt=x}=(es⁢(P-I))x⁢y.

Proof.
ℙ⁢{Xt+s=y∣Xt=x} =𝔼⁢[(PJ)x⁢y]
=ℙ⁢{Mj=y∣M0=x}=𝔼⁢[ℙ⁢{Mj=y∣M0=x,J}]
=∑j≥0ℙ{J=j}×ℙ{Mj=y∣M0=x,J=j}
=∑j≥0e-s⁢sjj!⁢(Pj)x⁢y
=e-s⁢(es⁢P)x⁢y=(es⁢(P-I))x⁢y

∎

Corollary 3.4.

The generator of (Xt)t≥0 is G=P-I (acting on f∈ℝn, i.e. f:[n]→ℝ).

Definition 3.5.

A Continuous-Time Markov Chain (CTMC) on 𝒳 is defined by its (infinitesimal) generator (matrix) G=(Gx⁢y)x,y∈𝒳, with Gx⁢y≥0 for all x≠y and ∑yGx⁢y=0.

Note.

In the above definition, Gx⁢y can be thought of as the “rate of jumps from x to y.”

If (Xt)t≥0 is a CTMC with generator G, then the transition probabilities are

ℙ⁢{Xt+s=y∣Xt=x}=(et⁢G)x⁢y
