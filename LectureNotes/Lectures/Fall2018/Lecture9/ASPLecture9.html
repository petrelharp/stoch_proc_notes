Cell Proliferation and Cancer

The following setup is the motivation for a later theorem on Hitting Probabilities. Mutations can affect the rates of cell proliferation (splitting) and death. Suppose we counted the number of occurences of a particular mutation in each of many tissue samples. We wonder if the distribution of mutation numbers is consistent with a driver mutation, i.e. one with a higher than normal rate of proliferation11 1 Here, normal would mean that the rate of cell proliferation equals the rate of cell death..

Model

Let Xt be the number of mutated cells at time t, so Xt∈ℕ≥0, and assume that X0=1 (so we start with one mutated cell).

Assume that each cell divides at rate λ and dies at rate μ. This means that

Xt →Xt+1 at rate λ⁢Xt
Xt →Xt-1 at rate μ⁢Xt.

Questions:

  1. 1.

    What is the probability that the mutation “grows?” That is, for some large N, what is

    ℙ⁡{Xt=N⁢ before ⁢Xt⁢ hits ⁢0}=?
  2. 2.

    If the mutation is not a driver but present due to an ongoing mutation of rate a, what is the expected distribution of the number of mutations per tissue sample?

Hitting Probabilities

To answer question 1 we need a bit more theory. Suppose (Xt)t≥0 is a Continuous Time Markov Chain with generator G on a state space 𝒳. For any subset A⊂𝒳, we define

τA=inf⁡{t≥0∣Xt∈A},

which is the first time that the Markov chain lands in A. If A={x} we just write τx, which is the first time the Markov chain hits x. We now state the theorem:

Theorem 1 (Harmonic Functions).

Suppose that A,B⊂X are disjoint subsets with the property that

ℙ⁡{τA∪B<∞}=1.

For x∈𝒳 we define

h⁢(x)=ℙx⁡{τA<τB}:=ℙ⁡{τA⁢<τB∣⁢X0=x}.

Then the function h is the unique function satisfying the following two conditions:

  1. 1.

    h⁢(x)={1 for ⁢x∈A0 for⁢x∈B

  2. 2.

    G⁢h⁢(x)=0 for x∉A∪B.

To prove the theorem we need two lemmas.

Lemma 1.

Let T1,…,Tn be independent random variables with Ti∼Exp⁢(λk). Let T:=min⁡{T1,…,Tn}. Then T∼Exp⁢(∑k=1nλk) and

ℙ⁡{Tk=T}=λkλ1+…+λn.
Proof of Lemma 1.
ℙ⁡{T>t} =ℙ⁡{Tk>t ∀k}
=∏k=1nℙ⁡{Tk>t} (by independence)
=∏k=1ne-λk⁢t
=e-t⁢∑kλk

which establishes the first claim. For the second claim, first note that

ℙ⁡{Tk∈d⁢t,Tj>t for ⁢j≠k} =λk⁢e-λk⁢t⁢∏j≠ke-λj⁢t
=λk⁢e-t⁢∑jλj,

so we now integrate over t to get

ℙ⁡{Tk=T}=λk⁢∫e-t⁢∑jλj⁢𝑑t=λkλ1+…+λn.

∎

Lemma 2.

Let τ+=inf⁡{t≥0∣Xt≠X0} be the time of the first jump. Then, if X0=x, then τ+∼Exp⁢(-Gx⁢x).

Lemma 3.

Let τ+=inf⁡{t≥0∣Xt≠X0} be the time of the first jump. Suppose ℙ⁡{τ+>0}>0. Then τ+∼Exp⁢(λ) for some λ>0.

Proof.

Let F⁢(t)=ℙ⁡{τ+>t} be the distribution function for τ+. By the Markov property on Xt, we have

ℙ⁡{τ+>t+s⁢∣τ+>⁢t}=ℙ⁡{τ+>s}=F⁢(s).

Unraveling the conditional probability on the left, we find

F⁢(s)=ℙ⁡{τ+>t+s⁢ and ⁢τ+>t}ℙ⁡{τ+>t}=F⁢(t+s)F⁢(t).

We then have that F⁢(t+s)=F⁢(t)⁢F⁢(s), and the only continuous solution to this equation is F⁢(t)=e-λ⁢t for some λ>0.

∎

To solve our problem with splitting and dying cells, we model it with a continuous time Markov chain with state space {1,2,…,N} and generator G given by

G=(00000…0μ-μ-λλ00…002⁢μ-2⁢μ-2⁢λ2⁢λ0…0003⁢μ-3⁢μ-3⁢λ3⁢λ…0⋮⋮)

Let h⁢(x)=ℙx⁡{τN<τ0} be the hitting probability. From the theorem on hitting probabilities, we know that

h⁢(0) =0
h⁢(N) =1
G⁢h⁢(x) =0 ∀x≠0,N.

The last of these equations says

x⁢λ⁢(h⁢(x+1)-h⁢(x))+x⁢μ⁢(h⁢(x-1)-h⁢(x))=0

which rearranges into

h⁢(x+1)-h⁢(x)=μλ⁢(h⁢(x)-h⁢(x-1)).

Since h⁢(1)-h⁢(0)=h⁢(1) by our boundary condition we can use this as the seed for this recurrence relation. We get

h⁢(x+1)-h⁢(x)=(μλ)x⁢h⁢(1),

so

h⁢(x) =h⁢(1)+∑y=1x-1h⁢(y+1)-h⁢(y)
=h⁢(1)⁢{1+∑y=1x-1(μλ)y}
=h⁢(1)⁢(1-(μλ)x1-μλ).

Next we use the other boundary condition:

1=h⁢(N)=h⁢(1)⁢(1-(μλ)N1-μλ)

which gives

h⁢(1)={1-(μλ)N1-μλ}-1,

and

h⁢(x)=1-(μλ)x1-(μλ)N.

This gives us the answer to our first question: what is the probability that a single mutation “grows?” This is answered by the value

h⁢(1)=1-(μλ)1-(μλ)N≈1-μλ⁢ for large ⁢N.

Stationary Distributions

Proposition 1.

Let (Xt)t≥0 be a continuous time Markov chain with generator G on state space 𝒳. Suppose that GT⁢π=0 for some probability distribution π on 𝒳. Then

ℙπ⁡{Xt=y}=π⁢(y).

The conditions on π above mean

∑y∈𝒳π⁢(y)=1,π⁢(y)≥0,∑y∈𝒳π⁢(y)⁢Gy⁢x=0.

The notation ℙπ means

ℙπ⁡{Xt=y}=∑x∈𝒳π⁢(x)⁢ℙx⁡{Xt=y}.

Such a distribution π is called a stationary distribution.

Proof.

Let f:𝒳→ℝ be a function (thought of as a column vector) with ∑yπ⁢(y)⁢f⁢(y)<∞ (if we wished to generalize to a countable state space). Recall that

𝔼x⁡[f⁢(Xt)]=Pt⁢f⁢(x)=et⁢G⁢f⁢(x).

Since π⁢G=0,

π⁢Pt=π⁢et⁢G=π⁢∑n≥0tnn!⁢Gn=π.

So,

𝔼π⁡[f⁢(Xt)]=π⁢Pt⁢f=π⁢f=∑xπ⁢(x)⁢f⁢(x).

The statement of the proposition now follows from taking f to be the indicator of y:

f⁢(x)={1x=y0x≠y.

∎

Fact 1.

If π is unique, then Pt→π as t→∞. That is, for all f,x,

𝔼x⁡[f⁢(Xt)]→∑yπ⁢(y)⁢f⁢(y) as ⁢t→∞.

This can also be phrased as saying that Xt converges in distribution to π.
